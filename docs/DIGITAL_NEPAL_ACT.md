## Part I: Definitions & Scope

### Article 1 — Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Soul Key** | A Merkle Tree-based cryptographic identity whose root is the hash of a citizen's life events. Formula: `Life Hash = H(Event_1 \|\| H(Event_2 \|\| H(Event_3 \|\| ...)))` |
| **Personal AI Twin** | A federated learning model that resides on the citizen's device, never exposing raw data to central servers |
| **Zero-Trust Bridge** | A cryptographic gateway that verifies every access request regardless of origin (internal or external) |
| **YetiCloud** | A distributed GPU cloud network using locally-sourced hardware within Nepal's borders |
| **Smart Contract Gig Economy** | A decentralized marketplace for short-term (5-30 day) contracts governed by smart contracts on a national blockchain |
| **Data Democratization** | The principle that citizens own and monetize their own data through cryptographic consent mechanisms |
| **Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC)** | Cryptographic algorithms resistant to quantum computer attacks, specifically CRYSTALS-Kyber for key encapsulation |

### Article 2 — Scope

This Act applies to:

1. All citizens of the Federal Democratic Republic of Nepal
2. All government agencies at federal, provincial, and local levels
3. All private entities operating within Nepal's jurisdiction
4. All foreign entities processing data of Nepali citizens
5. All AI systems deployed for public service delivery

## Part II: Four Pillars of Digital Nepal

### Article 3 — Pillar 1: Citizen & Edge Layer

The Citizen & Edge Layer shall consist of:

1. **Personal AI Twin** — A locally-executing AI model that:
   - Learns from citizen's behavior patterns without uploading raw data
   - Uses Federated Learning to contribute aggregate insights to national models
   - Provides personalized government service recommendations
   - Operates under the principle of **Privacy by Design**

2. **Soul Key Identity** — A cryptographic identity anchored to:
   - Birth registration (first Merkle leaf)
   - Citizenship certificate
   - National Identity Card (NID)
   - Educational credentials
   - Land ownership records
   - Health records
   - Tax compliance history
   - Marriage registration
   - Passport
   - Driver's license
   - Voter registration
   - Pension enrollment

3. **Edge Device Security** — Mandatory:
   - TPM 2.0 hardware attestation for all government-issued devices
   - Biometric authentication (fingerprint + facial recognition)
   - Hardware-bound key storage
   - Automated lockout on unauthorized access attempts

### Article 4 — Pillar 2: Zero-Trust Security Bridge

The Zero-Trust Security Bridge shall implement:

1. **Never Trust, Always Verify** — Every API call, internal or external, must:
   - Present a valid cryptographic proof of identity
   - Pass through real-time risk assessment
   - Be logged to an immutable audit trail

2. **Post-Quantum Cryptography** — All communications must use:
   - CRYSTALS-Kyber for key encapsulation (NIST-standardized PQC)
   - Dilithium for digital signatures
   - Hybrid classical-PQC handshake during transition period

3. **Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKP)** — Citizens may prove:
   - Age > 18 without revealing birthdate
   - Income > threshold without revealing exact amount
   - Residency in province without revealing exact address
   - Tax compliance without revealing tax returns

4. **Automated Lockout Protocol** — On detecting unauthorized access:
   - **Stage 1**: Session revocation on blockchain
   - **Stage 2**: Data self-encryption with key escrow
   - **Stage 3**: NCSC incident registration
   - **Stage 4**: Citizen notification via multiple channels

### Article 5 — Pillar 3: Core Platform & Microservices

The Core Platform shall consist of:

1. **National Identity Service** — Soul Key issuance, verification, and recovery
2. **Consensus Engine** — Federated Byzantine Agreement for national blockchain
3. **Government Action Layer** — Digital submission, approval, and tracking of all government actions
4. **Economy Engine** — Digital rupee (e-Rupee) transactions, taxation, and treasury management
5. **Mesh Network** — Decentralized peer-to-peer communication resilient to infrastructure failure
6. **AI Service Mesh** — Federated learning coordination, model distribution, and inference routing
7. **Smart Contract Registry** — Legally-binding smart contracts for:
   - Government procurement
   - Land registration
   - Business licensing
   - Employment contracts (5-30 day gig economy)
   - Supply chain tracking

### Article 6 — Pillar 4: Infrastructure Layer

The Infrastructure Layer shall provide:

1. **YetiCloud** — National GPU cloud:
   - Distributed across all 7 provinces
   - Locally-sourced hardware (Nepali assembly preferred)
   - Priority allocation for government AI workloads
   - Excess capacity sold to private sector

2. **CDN Edge Network** — Content delivery:
   - Edge nodes in all 77 districts
   - Cached government services, forms, and AI models
   - Offline-first architecture for low-connectivity regions

3. **National Blockchain** — Permissioned blockchain:
   - 51% Government + 49% Private Sector consortium
   - Validator nodes in each province
   - Public auditability of government transactions
   - Private channels for sensitive citizen data

4. **Disaster Recovery** — Geo-redundant:
   - Primary data center in Kathmandu Valley
   - Secondary in Pokhara
   - Tertiary in Janakpur
   - Mesh-based fallback for critical services

## Part III: Governance & Consortium

### Article 7 — National Digital Council

A **National Digital Council (NDC)** shall be established comprising:

| Seat | Entity | Voting Power |
|------|--------|-------------|
| 1 | Ministry of Communication & IT | 15% |
| 2 | Ministry of Home Affairs | 10% |
| 3 | Ministry of Finance | 10% |
| 4 | National Planning Commission | 8% |
| 5 | Nepal Rastra Bank (Central Bank) | 8% |
| 6 | Private Sector Consortium (5 seats) | 49% (9.8% each) |

**Total: 51% Government / 49% Private Sector**

### Article 8 — Consortium Membership

Private sector consortium members must:

1. Be registered in Nepal with minimum 51% Nepali ownership
2. Pass security clearance by the Ministry of Home Affairs
3. Contribute to YetiCloud infrastructure proportional to voting power
4. Submit to annual independent security audit
5. Comply with Data Sovereignty Clause (Article 12)

### Article 9 — Oversight & Compliance

1. **Independent Auditor** — Appointed by the Supreme Court
2. **Citizen Grievance Portal** — Digital mechanism for reporting violations
3. **Annual Transparency Report** — Published by NDC
4. **Parliamentary Oversight Committee** — Quarterly review

## Part IV: Legal Framework

### Article 10 — Data Sovereignty Clause

1. All citizen data generated within Nepal's borders is subject to Nepali jurisdiction
2. Cross-border data transfer requires:
   - Explicit citizen consent via Soul Key signature
   - Encryption with Nepali-government-approved algorithms
   - Audit trail accessible to citizen
3. Foreign entities processing Nepali citizen data must:
   - Register with the NDC
   - Maintain a data processing facility within Nepal
   - Appoint a local data protection officer

### Article 11 — Right to Self-Sovereign Identity (SSI)

1. Every citizen has the right to a Soul Key
2. The Soul Key is the sole property of the citizen, not the state
3. The state may verify Soul Key signatures but cannot access the private key
4. Citizens may generate unlimited pseudonymous identities derived from their Soul Key
5. Soul Key recovery mechanism must involve multi-party computation (MPC) with:
   - Citizen's biometric
   - Two designated family members
   - One government registrar

### Article 12 — Cryptographic Proof Validity

1. Zero-Knowledge Proofs are legally equivalent to document-based proofs
2. Smart contracts executed on the national blockchain are legally binding
3. Digital signatures via Soul Key have the same legal weight as handwritten signatures
4. Blockchain timestamps are admissible as evidence in courts
5. The National Digital Council shall maintain a list of approved cryptographic algorithms

### Article 13 — Smart Contract Regulation

1. Smart contracts for government procurement must be:
   - Written in a formally-verifiable language (e.g., Vyper, Rust)
   - Audited by NDC-approved third party
   - Deployed on the national blockchain
   - Monitored for automatic compliance

2. Gig economy smart contracts (5-30 days) must include:
   - Minimum wage compliance
   - Social security contribution
   - Dispute resolution mechanism
   - Automatic termination conditions

3. Smart contract disputes shall be resolved by:
   - Automated arbitration (first tier)
   - Digital Tribunal (second tier)
   - Supreme Court (final appeal)

## Part V: Economic Framework

### Article 14 — Digital Rupee (e-Rupee)

1. The Nepal Rastra Bank shall issue a Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC)
2. The e-Rupee shall be programmable for:
   - Conditional transfers (e.g., scholarship funds usable only for education)
   - Automatic tax deduction at source
   - Time-locked payments
   - Multi-signature approval for government spending

### Article 15 — Data Monetization

1. Citizens may license their anonymized data through the Soul Key protocol
2. Data buyers must:
   - Register with NDC
   - Specify purpose limitation
   - Pay licensing fees to citizen directly
   - Destroy data after purpose fulfillment

3. The state may not monetize citizen data without explicit consent
4. Aggregate anonymized data for public policy is exempt from consent but must be:
   - Published openly
   - Not re-identifiable
   - Reviewed by ethics committee

### Article 16 — YetiCloud Economics

1. YetiCloud compute credits are tradeable on the national marketplace
2. GPU providers (individuals with spare GPU capacity) may:
   - Register their hardware
   - Receive compute credits
   - Convert credits to e-Rupee or tax deductions

3. Priority pricing:
   - Government AI workloads: 50% discount
   - Education & research: 30% discount
   - Private sector: Market rate

## Part VI: Security & Emergency Protocols

### Article 17 — National Cyber Security Center (NCSC)

1. All security incidents must be reported to NCSC within 24 hours
2. NCSC maintains:
   - National vulnerability database
   - Incident response team (CSIRT)
   - Threat intelligence sharing platform
   - Security audit framework

### Article 18 — Automated Lockout Protocol

On detection of unauthorized access to a citizen's Soul Key:

1. **Immediate**: Session revocation across all government services
2. **Within 1 hour**: Data self-encryption with key escrow
3. **Within 24 hours**: NCSC incident registration
4. **Within 48 hours**: Citizen notification via:
   - SMS
   - Email
   - Physical letter (registered post)
   - In-person at local ward office

### Article 19 — Emergency Override

1. The Prime Minister may authorize emergency override of Soul Key locks with:
   - Supreme Court approval (within 72 hours)
   - Parliamentary notification (within 7 days)
   - Full public disclosure (within 30 days)

2. Emergency override is limited to:
   - National security threats
   - Natural disaster response
   - Public health emergencies
